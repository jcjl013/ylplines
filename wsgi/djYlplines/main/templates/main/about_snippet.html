All About Ylplines


What Is It

Ylplines seeks to visualize the timeline of Yelp ratings for the businesses we have visited and also businesses we're looking to visit. Along the way, why not come up with a more precise rating for businesses, as well? Coupling these goals, ylplines serves to amuse and enlighten us on how has a business performed over the years.

Check out the short-term and long-term trends of a particular business. It's pretty fun to look up your favorite places and see how it's fared over the years.

Ylplines communicates directly with Yelp and its API in order to provide data analysis and visualization. We do not passively web crawl or deploy spiders on Yelp, which means ylplines pulls information on demand. What that translates to for the user is that it does take time to get data about the business that's being looked up.

Ylplines is open sourced and licensed under the GNU General Public License agreement.

There are big ideas on how to improve the services ylplines provides that I'm excited to implement. This is a project that I've had a lot of fun working on, and I hope to continue improving on the fidelity and quality of ylplines for more continued programming fun.



Methodology For Ylplines Ratings

Ylplines uses its own ratings algorithm to estimate the quality of a business. Ylplines does display the original Yelp rating as a reference when you're viewing a business, though.

Our ylpline rating is adjusted to put more weight on more recent reviews, employing the concept of exponential smoothing. As reviews grow older, they are weighted less towards the final rating. The decay factor ylplines employs can change often, and currently is still being trained over the course of the next few weeks.

We take into account every Yelp review for the business, date them, employ Ylplines' rating algorithm, and assess a final, normalized rating for what we think represents the current quality of a business based on Yelp reviews.



Technologies and Thanks

Ylplines's backbone uses the Django framework for quick and clear project organization. This obviously means the backend is written in Python with a PostgreSQL database, and the frontend makes ample use of Javascript and jQuery.

Ylplines source is stored in its safe space repo on Github. Travis CI for continuous integration. Coveralls for deployment testing . Waffle for work-tracking. Openshift for deployment.

And special thanks to all the other open source developers out there that share their super useful projects so that we don't all reinvent wheels.
